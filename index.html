Morningside Heights has a poop problem

Could it be due to a lack of trash cans? I walked my neighborhood to find out.

By Pete Brown

August 7, 2022

Dog poop is not just unsightly. It's also a health hazard. And it's a growing problem in New York City.

New Yorkers' failure to clean up after their dogs has become such a scourge that Council member Erik Botcher recently introduced a citywide public information campaign on LinkNYC machines and online.

@ebottcher's useful reminder to New Yorkers.

On a recent walk around Morningside Heights I noticed a startling amount of poop on the sidewalks.

What was particularly disheartening was the frequency with which those poops were within close proximity of a trash can.

A poop left by someone that is not a good neighbor.

I wanted to gauge the extent of the problem, so I took a walk around my neighborhood on a warm Sunday afternoon in July.

Armed with a map and pencil, I walked up and down every street from La Salle Street (124th) to 120th Street between Amsterdam Av. and Broadway, noting the location of every poop and trash can along the way.

The results were, well, disgusting.

19 poops were counted on La Salle Street alone.

I counted a total of 54 poops in this relatively small corner of the neighborhood.

So is the problem a lack of trash cans? 🗑

The answer, sadly, appears to be no.

I counted 27 trash cans in the same area – one for every second poop left on the sidewalk.

As you can see, they were available on almost every street corner.

So what can be done?

There are already laws in places to deter dog owners from leaving poop in public spaces.

NYC's 'curb your dog' penalty was introduced in 1978. It carries a fine of $250 for anyone failing to remove dog poop.

The current law, which carries a fine of $250.

Residents of Morningside Heights would be excused for wondering if it's time to try something new.
